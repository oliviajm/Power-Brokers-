Oakland · Public Education

Oakland spends $25,634 per student. Why can only 27% do math?

Because most of the money never reaches the classroom. A bloated central office, 77 school sites serving a 34,000-student district built for 54,000, and a $100M state bailout that took 22 years to repay have created a system that feeds itself while students fall further behind.

The result: a district that spends 60% more per student than the state average while producing students who perform 30% worse.

The Schools

77 school sites for 34,000 students. The district was built for 54,000. Half-empty buildings drain maintenance budgets while children crowd into the schools that remain.

The Results

Despite spending $25,634 per student, Oakland Unified produces some of the worst academic outcomes in California. The data tells a story of systemic failure at every level.

The Bailout

In 2003, Oakland Unified went insolvent. The state loaned $100M, the largest school bailout in California history. It took 22 years to repay. One month after the final payment: a new $30M deficit.

    The Spiral

    Enrollment decline means less state funding. Less funding means cuts. Cuts mean worse schools. Worse schools mean more families leave. The death spiral feeds itself while admin costs stay fixed.

    Who Benefits

    The adults in the system are protected. The kids are not. Admin salary bloat, teacher contract expansions, consulting contracts, and political pressure that prevents consolidation all serve the people who run the district, not the students it exists to educate.

    What Now

    $102.5M in cuts approved December 2025. FCMAT is watching. The risk of a second state takeover looms. OUSD would be the first California district to lose local control within a year of regaining it.
