Do teachers' unions protect sexual predators?

In California, it costs up to $450,000 and can take a decade to fire one teacher. LAUSD has paid $700 million+ in sexual abuse settlements. The union contract that makes this possible was just flagged by the federal government.

The result: a system where union-negotiated protections make it nearly impossible to remove predators, taxpayers fund the settlements, and children pay the price.

The System

How California's tenure protections create a fortress around teachers accused of misconduct — and why it costs half a million dollars to breach it.

The Transfers

"Dance of the Lemons." "Passing the Trash." The practice of transferring accused teachers to new schools instead of firing them — where they find new victims.

The Cover-Up

Mark Berndt taught at Miramonte Elementary for 32 years. He fed children cookies laced with his semen. Complaints were ignored for three decades.

    The Bill

    $700M+ in settlements. $1B+ in taxpayer bonds. Every dollar comes from the same budget meant for classrooms, teachers, and student services.

    The Rubber Rooms

    LAUSD had actual rooms where accused teachers sat in cubicles, collecting full pay, doing no work, for months or years while their cases crawled through the system.

    The Investigation

    The federal government is now investigating LAUSD for policies that "appear to automatically reassign teachers accused of sexual misconduct."

      What Now

      The union angle: CTA/UTLA-negotiated tenure protections created this system. The courts upheld it. The federal government is investigating it. The bonds will cost taxpayers over a billion dollars. And the pattern continues.
